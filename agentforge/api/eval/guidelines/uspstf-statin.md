# USPSTF Statin Use for Primary Prevention of Cardiovascular Disease in Adults (2022)

Source URL: https://www.uspreventiveservicestaskforce.org/uspstf/recommendation/statin-use-in-adults-preventive-medication

## Recommendation Summary

The USPSTF recommends that adults aged 40 to 75 years who have one or more cardiovascular disease (CVD) risk factors (i.e., dyslipidemia, diabetes, hypertension, or smoking) AND an estimated 10-year CVD risk of 10% or greater should initiate a statin for primary prevention of CVD events. This is a B recommendation.

## Initiation Criteria

For adults aged 40 to 75 with no history of CVD, statin therapy is recommended when ALL of the following are present: at least one of the four CVD risk factors (dyslipidemia, diabetes mellitus, hypertension, current smoking) AND a 10-year ASCVD risk score of 10% or greater. The pooled cohort equations (PCE) are the recommended risk calculator. Patients with type 2 diabetes mellitus aged 40-75 generally meet the criteria when other modifiable risk factors are present.

## Selective Offering

For adults aged 40 to 75 with at least one CVD risk factor and a 10-year CVD risk between 7.5% and less than 10%, the USPSTF recommends that clinicians selectively offer a statin. The benefit is smaller in this group; shared decision-making should weigh patient preference, comorbidities, and the magnitude of risk-factor burden. This is a C recommendation.

## Older Adults

The current evidence is insufficient to assess the balance of benefits and harms of initiating a statin for primary prevention of CVD events in adults 76 years or older. This is an I statement. For patients already on a statin who are tolerating it well at age 76 or older, continuation is generally reasonable based on shared decision-making.

## Statin Intensification in Diabetes

For adults with type 2 diabetes mellitus, the 2018 ACC/AHA Cholesterol Clinical Practice Guideline recommends moderate-intensity statin therapy. Patients with diabetes plus additional ASCVD risk enhancers (LDL-C 160 mg/dL or higher, family history of premature ASCVD, persistent elevations of triglycerides 175 mg/dL or higher, or metabolic syndrome) should be considered for high-intensity statin therapy. An LDL-C level of 158 mg/dL in a patient with diabetes and at least one additional risk enhancer typically warrants intensification from moderate-intensity to high-intensity statin therapy.

## Monitoring and Follow-up

After statin initiation or dose change, repeat lipid panel in 4 to 12 weeks to assess adherence and response. The expected reduction with high-intensity statin therapy is approximately 50% from baseline LDL-C. Continued non-response after dose escalation should prompt evaluation for adherence, secondary causes of dyslipidemia, and consideration of ezetimibe add-on therapy.

## Adverse Effects

Common adverse effects include myalgia (1-10%), elevated hepatic transaminases (less than 1%), and a small absolute increase in incident type 2 diabetes risk in patients with elevated baseline glycemia. Routine baseline liver function testing is reasonable; ongoing routine monitoring is not required in the absence of symptoms.

## Shared Decision-Making

Clinicians should engage patients in shared decision-making about statin therapy, presenting estimated absolute risk reduction over 10 years, potential adverse effects, the need for daily medication, and patient values. Patients with diabetes and an LDL-C above 130 mg/dL who decline initial moderate-intensity statin therapy benefit from documented counseling and follow-up reassessment within 6 to 12 months.
